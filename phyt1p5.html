1.3 Motion: Forces, Weight, Velocity and Free Fall.

Force is a push or pull. It is measured in Newton using force meter. It is applied on the object and can affect in many ways. For example, it could:

‚Ä¢ Make a stationery object move

‚Ä¢ Make a moving object stop, speed up, slow down, change direction (accelerate)

‚Ä¢ Change shape of the object (e.g. stretch, compress, break)

    ‚ùñ Force is a vector. It has a magnitude and direction
    ‚ùñ When a single force is applied to an object of certain mass, it causes an acceleration in the same direction of the force.
    ‚ùñ The equation relating the force applied, the mass of the object and the acceleration is f=ma.
    ‚ùñ Forces can act in different directions since it is a vector and multiple forces can act on the same object.
    ‚ùñ Forces may include friction, weight, magnetic force, air resistance, tension, thrust, water resistance.
    ‚ùñ Water resistance, air resistance and friction are all called drag forces and they act to the opposite direction of motion. For example, if a boat in the sea is moving towards the left, the water resistance would act upon the boat in the opposite direction which is towards the right. Another example would be a stone falling freely downwards. The stone is moving downwards, so the air resistance acting upon the stone would act upwards since it is the opposite direction of motion.
    ‚ùñ Weight is also called gravitational force and it is defined as the force attracting objects to the centre of the earth or pulling them downwards
    ‚ùñ Since multiple forces can act on the same object, we should be familiar with something called the resultant force. The resultant force is the single force which will have the same effect as the multiple forces acting on the object. For example, if a force of 5N is applied to a box pushing it in the left direction and another force of 9N is applied to the same box pushing it in the right direction. The resultant force would be the net force of these two forces. So, the resultant force would be 4N to the right. (9 to right ‚Äì 5 to left = 4 to right).
    ‚ùñ If a single force acts on a stationery object in a certain direction, it would result in the motion of the object with an acceleration in the same direction as the force.
    ‚ùñ If a single force acts on a moving object in the same direction of motion, the object will accelerate in that same direction of motion and force.
    ‚ùñ If a single force acts on a moving object in a different direction from the direction of motion, the object will decelerate.
    ‚ùñ As a GCSE or IGCSE student, you should be able to know the magnitude and the direction of the resultant force when there are two forces acting in different planes when, for example, a force of 3N acts on a point towards the north and a force of 4N acts on the same point towards the east. Since they are acting in different planes, you cannot simply subtract or add them together. Instead, you should draw a parallelogram of forces to determine the magnitude and direction of the resultant force. (diagrams shown below for a better understanding)

    ‚ùñ A free fall is the movement of the object under the force of gravity or weight. A free-falling object is not affected by any other forces such as air resistance or water resistance. The only force affecting a free-falling object is its weight. A free-falling object would move by an acceleration of 9.8 ùíé/ùíî^ ùüê which is equal to the acceleration due to gravity.
    ‚ùñ In practical life though, there is always air resistance affecting the object that is falling. But in theoretical physics question, we sometimes then ignore the air resistance to the object because it may be very minimal.
    ‚ùñ However, you should be able to know the motion of objects falling under the effect of both gravity and air resistance. You should be able to interpret the graphs of their motion and explain it.
    ‚ùñ Once an object falls, the only force affecting it is gravity. This would cause the object to accelerate by 9.8 ùíé/ùíî^ ùüê . However, as the speed of the object increases, air resistance also increases. The air resistance keeps increasing and the acceleration of the object will decrease. This means that the object speed will still increase but by a less amount in the same time period. The air resistance would keep increasing until it becomes equal to the weight. At this point of motion, there is no resultant force. This in turn means that the acceleration becomes 0. the object now is moving at its maximum constant velocity called the terminal velocity. Remember that an object moving in a circle will always have non-uniform (non-constant) velocity even if it is moving with a constant speed. That is because the direction is changed and velocity is defined by directions since it is a vector
